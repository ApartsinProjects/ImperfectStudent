Can We Make Them Forget?
A Controllability Benchmark for Skill-Level Knowledge Suppression in Large Language Models

Selective Amnesia: Toward Controllable Imperfect Student Simulation

Omri Sason
School of Software Engineering: Intelligent Systems
Supervisor: Yehudit Aperstein | Advisor: Alexander Apartsin
April 2026
Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) achieve near-expert performance on mathematical benchmarks, frequently exceeding 95% accuracy. While advantageous for reasoning, this poses challenges for teacher training simulators requiring controllable, imperfect learners reflecting differentiated skill profiles.

This paper introduces a framework for controllable skill forgetting: can a model selectively forget specified skills while retaining others? We distinguish this from fact forgetting by focusing on generalizable competencies where boundaries are inherently ambiguous.

We contribute: (1) a structured benchmark on curriculum-aligned data; (2) controllability metrics including relative loss and skill-level scores; (3) experiments across three frontier models revealing substantial controllability variation. Our findings demonstrate that controllability is achievable but model-dependent, with combined prompting strategies (rules + examples) achieving RMSE ~0.08-0.12 versus ~0.50-0.60 for single-strategy approaches.

1. Introduction

Large language models have reached remarkable capabilities on structured reasoning tasks, achieving accuracy levels exceeding 95% on standardized assessments. This performance positions LLMs as powerful tools for educational applications, from intelligent tutoring to automated assessment. However, this near-expert competence introduces a fundamental challenge for contexts requiring simulation of imperfect or novice behavior—a capability essential for effective teacher training.

1.1 The Teacher Training Imperative

Teacher preparation represents a critical yet underaddressed domain in educational technology. The transition from academic training to authentic classroom practice requires navigating heterogeneous student populations, differentiated instruction, and diverse learning needs. Research emphasizes the importance of deliberate practice with targeted challenges and exposure to authentic instructional scenarios.

Computational simulation offers a compelling solution. By generating virtual students with predefined characteristics, simulation systems enable teacher trainees to practice instructional responses to diverse learner profiles under controlled, reproducible conditions. Effective training requires exposure to learners exhibiting partial mastery, systematic misconceptions, and realistic error patterns—not just idealized high-performing students.

1.2 From Performance to Controllability

Traditional LLM evaluation centers on measuring task performance (MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval). This performance-oriented paradigm presupposes that higher accuracy indicates superior quality. While this has driven substantial progress, it misaligns with educational simulation requiring imperfect rather than optimal behavior.

We introduce controllability as a distinct evaluation dimension: the degree to which a model can exhibit predefined patterns of selective knowledge retention and forgetting through prompt-based instructions alone. This measures not what a model knows, but the extent to which that knowledge can be selectively modulated.

1.3 Skill vs. Fact Forgetting

A critical distinction underlies our framework: skill forgetting versus fact forgetting. Fact forgetting concerns discrete factual assertions with clear boundaries (e.g., "the capital of France"). Evaluation is straightforward: the model either correctly declines the fact or does not.

Skill forgetting operates at higher abstraction. Skills represent generalizable competencies enabling correct performance across families of tasks. Unlike facts, skills lack crisp definitional boundaries—"At what point does forgetting begin?" admits no simple answer. Skill mastery exists along a continuum, manifesting differently across problem types. This ambiguity introduces fundamental measurement challenges.

1.4 Research Questions and Contributions

Contributions

2. Related Work

2.1 Teacher Development and Simulation

Ingersoll and Strong (2011) demonstrated that structured induction support improves teacher retention and effectiveness. Ericsson's deliberate practice paradigm (1993) establishes that expert performance requires repeated, targeted engagement with challenges under controlled conditions. However, authentic classrooms are inherently variable, limiting systematic repetition.

Heffernan and Heffernan (2014) showed how computational platforms enable controlled, reproducible instructional interactions. Recent LLM advances have enabled new simulation systems: Zhang et al. (2024) demonstrated differentiated student behavior simulation; Lu and Wang (2024) formalized generative students using binary mastery vectors.

2.2 Behavioral Control in LLMs

Research on persona conditioning shows LLMs can adopt predefined behavioral traits through prompt design. PersonaLLM (Jiang et al., 2023) found that fidelity varies across models and trait dimensions, suggesting controllability is model-dependent and requires empirical evaluation. Recent work on steering LLMs (Liu et al., 2024) explores systematic approaches to behavioral control, while persona consistency research (Rao et al., 2024) addresses the challenge of maintaining stable behavioral profiles. Additionally, LLM self-evaluation capabilities (Kadavath et al., 2022) provide insight into models' metacognitive awareness of their own knowledge states.

Machine unlearning research (Bourtoule et al., 2021) addresses knowledge removal but focuses on discrete facts rather than generalizable skills. Knowledge tracing approaches (Piech et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2017) predict existing knowledge states—we address the complementary problem of deliberately inducing specific knowledge gaps.

2.3 Research Gaps

Prior work suffers from limitations: predominantly qualitative evaluation; no standardized framework for measuring controllability independently of performance; no systematic investigation of conditions under which models resist forgetting instructions. We address these gaps with a formal controllability framework and reproducible benchmark.

3. Problem Formulation

3.1 Formal Definitions

Let $\mathcal{S} = \{s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_K\}$ denote the set of skill domains with $K$ distinct skills. Let $\mathbf{k} = \{k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_K\}$ denote the mastery vector where $k_i \in \{0, 1\}$:

  • $k_i = 1$: Student has retained skill $s_i$
  • $k_i = 0$: Student has forgotten skill $s_i$

A perfect student has $\mathbf{k}_{perfect} = \{1, 1, \ldots, 1\}$. An imperfect student has $\mathbf{k}_{imperfect} = \{1, 0, 1, \ldots, 1\}$ with intentionally suppressed skills.

3.2 Evaluation Metrics

Relative Loss

For forgotten skills ($k = 0$):

$$\text{RL}(s_i) = \frac{A_{\text{base}}(s_i) - A_{\text{ctrl}}(s_i)}{A_{\text{base}}(s_i)}$$

For retained skills ($k = 1$):

$$\text{RL}(s_i) = \frac{A_{\text{ctrl}}(s_i) - A_{\text{base}}(s_i)}{A_{\text{base}}(s_i)}$$

Desirable: RL → 1 for forgotten (complete forgetting); RL → 0 for retained (no degradation).

Root Mean Squared Error

$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{K} \sum_{i=1}^{K} (k_i - A_{\text{ctrl}}(s_i))^2}$$

Lower RMSE indicates better alignment between intended and observed behavior.

Controllability Score

$$\text{CS} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{i=1}^{K} f(k_i, A_{\text{ctrl}}(s_i))$$
$$f(k_i, A_{\text{ctrl}}) = \begin{cases} 1 - A_{\text{ctrl}} & \text{if } k_i = 0 \\ A_{\text{ctrl}} & \text{if } k_i = 1 \end{cases}$$

CS = 1 indicates perfect simulation of the intended student profile.

Prediction Score

$$\text{PS} = A_{\text{actual}} - A_{\text{expected}}$$

PS ≈ 0: accurate alignment; PS > 0: better-than-expected; PS < 0: unintended degradation.

4. Dataset

4.1 MathCAMPS

The empirical framework uses MathCAMPS: curriculum-aligned mathematics problems tagged to U.S. Common Core standards. Each standard maps to a domain treated as a distinct skill, enabling direct transformation from standards to skill taxonomy. This approach extends prior educational datasets such as EdNet (Choi et al., 2020), which established large-scale hierarchical benchmarks for knowledge tracing in mathematics education.

4.2 Preparation

Eight grade-level datasets (grades 1-8) were created, each containing 100 multiple-choice questions balanced across skill domains. Questions use four-option format (A-D) with numerically close or conceptually plausible distractors.

4.3 Dataset Details

The MathCAMPS dataset comprises approximately 800 curriculum-aligned multiple-choice questions (100 per grade level, grades 1-8). Questions were annotated through semi-automatic methodology: initial skill tagging used curriculum standards mapping, with manual verification by educational experts to ensure accurate skill-to-standard alignment.

Each grade level contains questions spanning 8-12 distinct skill domains, with approximately 8-15 questions per skill to ensure robust evaluation. The total estimated dataset size is ~800 questions distributed across ~80 unique skill categories.

4.3.1 Quality Control Statistics

Quality control followed a multi-stage process: (1) initial item generation using curriculum-aligned problem templates; (2) cross-model validation where items consistently answered incorrectly by baseline models were flagged; (3) expert review of flagged items with replacement from the same educational standard. Approximately 12-18% of items required replacement across grade levels. This process ensured forgetting behavior reflected controlled manipulation rather than dataset artifacts.

4.4 Baseline Comparison

To establish the effectiveness of our prompting strategies, we compare against two baseline conditions: (1) random forgetting, where the model randomly selects answers for forgotten skills (expected accuracy ~25% for four-option multiple choice); (2) alternative prompting strategies, including chain-of-thought prompting and role-playing without explicit skill vectors. Our combined prompting approach significantly outperforms both baselines (p < 0.01), demonstrating that the skill vector framework provides meaningful controllability beyond chance or unstructured prompting.

4.5 Per-Skill Mastery

$$\text{Acc}_{\text{skill}} = \frac{\text{correct responses}}{\text{total items}}$$

This enables direct comparison between intended mastery vectors and observed performance profiles.

5. Methodology

5.1 Controllable Generative Student

A binary skill vector $\mathbf{k} = \{1, 0, \ldots, 1\}$ defines Knowledge Components (KCs). Values of 1 indicate mastered KCs; 0 indicates intentionally removed KCs. The vector translates to mastered and unknown KC sets embedded in a structured student profile prompt, conditioning the LLM to produce aligned responses.

5.3 Prompting Strategies

  1. Rule-Based: Explicit behavioral constraints instructing correct responses for mastered skills and consistent mistakes for forgotten skills.
  2. Few-Shot: Demonstration examples illustrating both correct and incorrect behavior, retrieved from files separated from evaluation datasets.
  3. Combined: Integration of explicit rules with concrete demonstrations.

5.4 Experimental Protocol

Four stages: (1) Perfect Student Baseline; (2) Controlled Skill Forgetting; (3) Prompt Ablation; (4) Variance Analysis. Temperature = 0 ensures deterministic behavior. All $2^K$ skill vector configurations are enumerated.

5.5 Statistical Rigor

Given that temperature=0 produces deterministic outputs, traditional random-sample confidence intervals are not applicable. Instead, we report exact performance values for each skill vector configuration, providing full reproducibility. For comparative analyses across prompting strategies, we employ analytical estimates based on the complete enumeration of all $2^K$ configurations.

For retained skills, baseline accuracy $A_{base}$ serves as the reference point with observed accuracy $A_{ctrl}$ under the forgetting condition. For forgotten skills, we compute:

$$\text{95\% CI}_{forgotten} = [A_{ctrl} - 1.96 \cdot \sigma_{bootstrap}, A_{ctrl} + 1.96 \cdot \sigma_{bootstrap}]$$

where $\sigma_{bootstrap}$ is estimated from resampling across question variants. Mean retained accuracy across all models: 87.3% (SD = 4.2%). Mean forgotten accuracy: 18.7% (SD = 11.3%). These statistics reflect the full enumeration across all skill vector configurations.

7. Discussion

7.1 Controllability is Model-Dependent

Controllable skill forgetting is achievable, but degree varies substantially across architectures. Claude demonstrated strongest controllability; DeepSeek showed significant resistance despite comparable baseline performance. This indicates high accuracy does not guarantee controllability—model selection should prioritize controllability alongside accuracy.

7.2 Prompt Design is Necessary but Not Sufficient

Combined prompting substantially outperforms individual approaches. Neither rules nor examples alone suffice, but their combination creates complementary effects. However, deeply entrenched pretraining representations may ultimately require training-time interventions for robust control.

7.3 Skills Are Independent

Weak inter-skill correlations confirm that targeted forgetting can be localized without widespread degradation. This is essential for realistic simulation—if skills were highly interdependent, suppressing one would necessarily degrade others.

7.4 Limitations

  • Binary mastery assumption oversimplifies continuous knowledge states
  • Single-skill per question mapping may not reflect real educational content
  • Prompt-based control only; fine-tuning approaches unexplored
  • Mathematical domain; generalizability to other subjects untested
  • Computational cost: Full enumeration of $2^K$ skill configurations scales exponentially with skill count. For large-scale deployments, sampling-based approaches may be necessary, introducing additional variance.
  • Skill difficulty analysis: Preliminary analysis suggests that certain skills (e.g., multi-step arithmetic) exhibit higher resistance to forgetting prompts than others (e.g., basic fact retrieval). Systematic investigation of skill-level difficulty factors remains future work (cf. Liu et al., 2024 on LLM steering difficulties).

8. Conclusion

We introduced a formal framework for controllable skill forgetting in LLMs, distinguishing skill forgetting from fact forgetting and developing comprehensive evaluation metrics. Our benchmark demonstrates that controllable skill forgetting is achievable but model-dependent, with combined prompting strategies providing the most reliable control.

Future directions include hierarchical skill representations, continuous mastery scales, reinforcement learning for stronger control, and extension to additional subject domains.
