# Introduction

The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has fundamentally transformed the landscape of artificial intelligence applications across numerous domains. Contemporary frontier models demonstrate remarkable capabilities on structured reasoning tasks, frequently achieving accuracy levels exceeding 95% on standardized mathematics assessments and professional examinations. This unprecedented performance has positioned LLMs as powerful tools for educational applications, ranging from intelligent tutoring systems to automated assessment platforms. However, this same characteristic of near-expert competence introduces a significant challenge when LLMs are deployed in contexts that require the simulation of imperfect or novice behavior—a capability that is essential for effective teacher training and pedagogical practice.

## The Teacher Training Imperative

Teacher preparation programs represent a critical yet often underaddressed domain in educational technology research. The transition from academic training to authentic classroom practice constitutes a formative phase in novice teachers' professional development, during which they must navigate the complexities of heterogeneous student populations, differentiated instruction, and diverse learning needs. Research on teacher induction consistently emphasizes the importance of structured mentorship, deliberate practice with targeted challenges, and exposure to authentic instructional scenarios for developing pedagogical expertise. However, authentic classroom environments are inherently variable and unpredictable, presenting significant constraints on the systematic repetition and controlled experimentation necessary for deliberate practice.

Computational simulation environments offer a compelling solution to this challenge. By generating virtual students with predefined characteristics, simulation systems enable teacher trainees to practice instructional responses to diverse learner profiles under controlled, reproducible conditions. Critically, effective teacher training requires exposure not only to idealized high-performing students but also to learners who exhibit partial mastery, systematic misconceptions, and realistic error patterns. A simulation system that generates only uniformly expert-level responses fails to prepare teachers for the authentic variability they will encounter in actual classrooms. Consequently, there exists a pressing need for generative student models that can reliably simulate differentiated skill profiles—specifically, students who demonstrate selective knowledge gaps that reflect authentic patterns of human learning and forgetting.

## From Performance Evaluation to Controllability Assessment

The evaluation paradigm for large language models has traditionally centered on measuring their ability to perform tasks correctly. Benchmark suites such as MMLU, GSM8K, and HumanEval assess model capabilities by quantifying accuracy, reasoning quality, and task completion rates. This evaluation framework presupposes that higher performance indicates superior model quality, and considerable research effort has been devoted to improving accuracy metrics through architectural innovations, training methodologies, and inference-time optimizations. While this performance-oriented paradigm has driven substantial progress in LLM capabilities, it creates a fundamental misalignment when applied to educational simulation contexts that require imperfect rather than optimal behavior.

In this paper, we introduce and formalize the concept of **controllability** as a distinct evaluation dimension for LLMs in educational applications. Controllability refers to the degree to which a model can be directed to exhibit predefined patterns of selective knowledge retention and forgetting through prompt-based instructions alone. This concept fundamentally differs from traditional performance metrics in that it measures not what a model knows, but rather the extent to which that knowledge can be selectively modulated in response to external specifications. An ideally controllable model would demonstrate perfect accuracy when instructed to retain a skill and zero accuracy when instructed to forget the same skill, thereby enabling precise simulation of any desired learner profile.

The distinction between controllability and raw performance becomes particularly evident when considering the internal representations that LLMs develop during pretraining. Contemporary models acquire deeply entrenched knowledge representations through exposure to vast corpora of human-generated text. When prompted to "forget" a specific skill or domain, these models frequently continue to solve related problems correctly—a phenomenon that reflects the robustness of pretraining knowledge rather than any deficiency in instruction-following capability. This observation reveals that accuracy and controllability represent orthogonal properties: a model may achieve high performance while remaining largely uncontrollable, and conversely, a controllable model need not achieve state-of-the-art accuracy on any particular task.

## Skill Forgetting Versus Fact Forgetting

A critical conceptual distinction underlies our framework: the difference between **skill forgetting** and **fact forgetting**. Fact forgetting, as explored in prior literature on machine unlearning and knowledge deletion, concerns the removal of specific factual assertions from model behavior—for instance, ensuring that a model no longer asserts that a particular individual was born in a given city. This type of forgetting operates on discrete, well-defined knowledge units with clear factual boundaries. The evaluation of fact forgetting is correspondingly straightforward: a model either correctly declines to state the targeted fact or it does not.

Skill forgetting, by contrast, operates at a higher level of abstraction and presents substantially greater conceptual and practical challenges. Skills represent generalizable capabilities or competencies—sets of related abilities that enable correct performance across a family of related tasks. A skill such as "solving linear equations" or "applying the Pythagorean theorem" encompasses numerous specific instantiations, each requiring the appropriate application of the underlying principle to different contexts. Unlike facts, skills lack crisp definitional boundaries. The question "At what point does forgetting begin?" admits no simple answer, as skill mastery exists along a continuum and manifests differently across varied problem types.

This ambiguity in skill definition introduces fundamental measurement challenges. When a student forgets a mathematical skill, their errors are not random but systematic—reflecting specific misconceptions about underlying principles. A simulation system that merely injects random errors fails to capture this structured nature of skill forgetting. Similarly, measuring whether a model has successfully forgotten a skill requires careful consideration of what constitutes successful forgetting: is it zero accuracy on all problems in a domain, or is some residual performance acceptable as long as errors exhibit the expected systematic patterns? Our framework addresses these challenges by grounding skill definitions in established educational taxonomies and developing metrics that capture both quantitative accuracy reduction and qualitative alignment with expected error patterns.

## Challenges in Controllable Skill Modulation

The realization of controllable skill forgetting through prompt-based instructions faces several interconnected challenges that distinguish it from conventional LLM evaluation tasks.

**Boundary Ambiguity** represents the first fundamental challenge. Unlike factual knowledge with clear demarcations, skills exist on a continuum and interact with one another in complex ways. A mathematical skill such as fraction manipulation draws upon more fundamental arithmetic skills, and "forgetting" fraction operations may have differential effects on different underlying competencies. Furthermore, skills exhibit varied granularity: "arithmetic" encompasses "addition," which encompasses "two-digit addition," which encompasses specific procedural steps. Determining the appropriate level of granularity for skill-level control remains an open question with implications for both the design of educational simulations and the interpretation of controllability metrics.

**Interference Effects** constitute the second challenge. When a model is instructed to forget one skill, unintended degradation may occur in retained skills—a phenomenon analogous to catastrophic interference in neural network training. Conversely, instruction to forget may paradoxically reinforce related knowledge through repetition or emphasis. Understanding and mitigating such interference effects requires careful experimental design and analysis.

**Evaluation Methodology** presents the third challenge. Traditional accuracy-based metrics fail to capture the nuanced nature of skill forgetting. A model that achieves 50% accuracy on a skill domain might be exhibiting either genuine forgetting or simply baseline difficulty with the material. Disentangling these possibilities requires comparison against carefully constructed baselines and the development of novel metrics that isolate controllability from inherent task difficulty.

**Model Resistance** represents a fourth challenge that emerges from the intrinsic characteristics of contemporary LLMs. Models trained with strong instruction-following objectives may resist behavioral modification that contradicts their internalized knowledge, particularly when that knowledge is deeply encoded through extensive pretraining. Understanding which model architectures and training paradigms facilitate or impede controllability is essential for practical deployment in educational contexts.

## Contributions

This paper makes the following primary contributions to the field of LLM-based educational simulation:

### A Structured Benchmark Framework

We introduce a comprehensive benchmark framework for evaluating controllable skill forgetting in LLMs, built upon the MathCAMPS curriculum-aligned dataset. This framework provides standardized test cases with explicit skill annotations, enabling reproducible evaluation across models and prompting strategies. The benchmark encompasses multiple grade levels and skill domains, ensuring broad coverage of mathematical competencies and facilitating analysis of controllability across varied task types.

### A Controllability Measurement Framework

We develop a principled measurement framework that distinguishes controllability from raw performance. This framework introduces several novel evaluation metrics, including a **relative loss formulation** that quantifies selective forgetting independent of baseline capability, a **skill-level controllability score** that captures adherence to predefined mastery vectors, and a **cross-skill influence matrix** that identifies unintended interference between skills. Together, these metrics provide a multidimensional assessment of how effectively a model can be directed to exhibit specified knowledge states.

### Empirical Benchmarking of Frontier Models

We conduct extensive experiments across multiple state-of-the-art LLMs, including Claude, DeepSeek, and GPT-4o, under varied prompting strategies. Our analysis reveals substantial variation in controllability across models, with some achieving near-perfect selective forgetting while others exhibit significant resistance or unintended interference. These findings provide actionable guidance for selecting models appropriate for educational simulation applications.

### A Formal Problem Formulation

We provide a rigorous mathematical formalization of the controllable skill forgetting problem, establishing clear definitions, assumptions, and evaluation criteria. This formalization enables precise reasoning about the properties of controllable student simulation and establishes a foundation for future research in this direction.
