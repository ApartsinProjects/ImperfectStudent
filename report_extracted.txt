Abstract
Large Language Models (LLMs) achieve near-expert performance on structured mathematical benchmarks, frequently exceeding 95% accuracy across grade-level assessments. While such performance is advantageous for reasoning applications, it poses a challenge for teacher preparation simulators. The transition from academic training to real classroom practice is a critical phase in novice teachers’ professional development, requiring exposure to heterogeneous classrooms, partial student mastery, and authentic error patterns that are rarely reflected in high-performing models. A classroom simulation environment designed for early-career teachers, therefore, requires controllable, imperfect learners that reflect differentiated skill profiles rather than uniformly high accuracy.
This master’s project introduces a formal framework for controllable generative students — LLM-based agents that simulate predefined patterns of skill retention and forgetting. We represent student knowledge using explicit binary skill vectors and design prompt-based control mechanisms to enforce selective mastery constraints. Unlike traditional evaluation paradigms that emphasize correctness, we propose controllability-oriented metrics that quantify whether a model forgets when instructed and retains when required, independently of its baseline performance. More specifically, we measure the extent to which the model can faithfully reflect the behavior of a student with learning gaps — not merely by lowering accuracy, but by exhibiting structured, skill-aligned deficiencies consistent with the predefined mastery vector.
Using structured MathCAMPS multi-grade datasets with explicit skill tagging, we conduct controlled experiments across multiple LLMs under different prompting strategies (rule-based, few-shot, and hybrid). We introduce a relative loss metric and a skill-level controllability score that capture selective compliance fidelity.
Results demonstrate that controllability is achievable and can be reliably induced, though its magnitude varies across prompting strategies and model architectures. The proposed framework establishes a reproducible methodology for evaluating skill-aware behavioral control in LLM-based educational simulations.

Introduction
Large Language Models have rapidly reached state-of-the-art performance across reasoning-intensive tasks, including structured mathematical assessments. Recent evaluations demonstrate that frontier models frequently achieve accuracy levels comparable to high-performing students.
However, high accuracy alone is insufficient for educational simulation.
Teacher-training systems require simulated learners who exhibit structured misconceptions, demonstrate partial mastery, make systematic domain-specific errors, and fail selectively and predictably. In addition, a computational simulation system enables precise repetition and replay of instructional sequences — a capability that is difficult to achieve in current human-based simulation settings — thereby supporting systematic reflection and controlled pedagogical experimentation.
In contrast, modern LLMs exhibit strong global competence. When instructed to “forget” a concept, they often continue to solve related problems correctly due to deeply internalized representations acquired during large-scale pretraining.
This mismatch exposes a fundamental gap: Accuracy is not equivalent to controllability.
This master’s project investigates the following central research question:
Can we configure a prompted LLM to selectively forget certain skills and knowledge while reliably retaining others?
To address this, we introduce the concept of a controllable generative student — an LLM agent governed by an explicit skill mastery profile that constrains its generative behavior.
1.1 Contributions
This work makes four primary contributions. First, it introduces a formal skill representation based on a binary skill-vector framework for explicit mastery specification. Second, it develops prompt-based control mechanisms that enforce selective retention and forgetting. Third, it proposes controllability-oriented metrics, including a relative loss formulation that separates compliance from baseline performance. Fourth, it defines a reproducible evaluation methodology across prompting strategies and models.


2. Theoretical Background and Literature Review
2.1 Induction of Novice Teachers and Opportunities for LLM Integration
The transition from academic preparation to real classroom teaching is widely recognized as a critical stage in novice teachers’ professional development. Research on induction programs highlights the importance of structured mentoring, guided reflection, peer collaboration, and emotional support in strengthening professional identity and reducing burnout (Ingersoll & Strong, 2011; Goldberg & Orland-Barak, 2016). Deliberate practice theory further emphasizes the need for repeated, structured engagement with targeted challenges under controlled conditions in order to refine expertise (Ericsson, Krampe, & Tesch-Römer, 1993). However, authentic classroom environments are inherently variable, limiting the ability to recreate identical instructional situations for repeated practice (Heffernan & Heffernan, 2014). This creates an opportunity for computational simulation environments that enable controlled repetition of pedagogical scenarios while maintaining curricular alignment.
2.2 LLM-Driven Simulations in Teacher Training
Recent advances in large language models have enabled the development of simulation environments in which virtual students respond according to predefined characteristics. Zhang et al. (2024) demonstrated that LLM-empowered classroom agents can simulate differentiated student behavior, including intentional errors and varying engagement levels. Similarly, Sun et al. (2024) highlighted the potential of LLM-driven educational simulations for scalable and personalized learning environments. These studies show qualitative alignment between predefined profiles and generated behavior, yet they do not systematically quantify the degree of behavioral adherence to a formally specified mastery structure.
2.3 Embedding Profiles and Personas in LLMs
Research on persona conditioning demonstrates that LLMs can adopt predefined behavioral traits through prompt design. PersonaLLM (Feng et al., 2023) showed that models can maintain stylistic and personality consistency across interactions, although perfect coherence remains challenging. In the educational domain, Lu and Wang (2024) introduced the concept of Generative Students, in which LLMs simulate learners with explicit skill profiles represented as binary mastery vectors. Their framework demonstrated that LLMs can condition responses on predefined knowledge states. Building on this foundation, the present project focuses specifically on controlled skill suppression and formal measurement of selective forgetting.
2.4 Knowledge Tracing and Skill Gap Identification
Knowledge Tracing models provide a probabilistic framework for estimating learner mastery over time. Bayesian Knowledge Tracing and its extensions model transitions between known and unknown states, while deep learning approaches such as Deep Knowledge Tracing (Piech et al., 2015), Dynamic Key-Value Memory Networks (Zhang et al., 2017), and Self-Attentive Knowledge Tracing (Pandey & Karypis, 2019) update skill states based on sequential interactions. Gervet et al. (2020) evaluated when deep models outperform classical approaches in diagnosing learner knowledge. More recently, SkillGapGPT (2025) leveraged LLMs to identify skill gaps directly from student responses, reporting strong alignment with expert annotations. These approaches focus on detecting or predicting knowledge states, whereas the present work focuses on deliberately inducing predefined knowledge gaps in a controlled simulation context.
2.5 Evaluation of Generative Student Behavior
Evaluation in educational modeling commonly relies on quantitative metrics such as accuracy, RMSE, AUC, precision, recall, and F1 score (Gervet et al., 2020; SkillGapGPT, 2025). These metrics assess predictive performance or alignment between inferred and true skill states. However, traditional evaluation paradigms emphasize correctness rather than controllability. The current project extends these quantitative evaluation principles by introducing a controllability-oriented formulation that measures the degree to which generated responses align with a predefined mastery vector, thereby distinguishing between raw performance and structured behavioral compliance.

4. Data Description
The empirical framework is built upon the MathCAMPS dataset, which contains curriculum-aligned mathematics problems tagged according to U.S. Common Core standards. Each educational standard is mapped to a Domain, and in the context of this project, each Domain is treated as a distinct Skill. This mapping enables a direct transformation from educational standards to a structured skill taxonomy suitable for controllable simulation.
To ensure fine-grained control and fair evaluation, the dataset was reorganized into eight separate grade-level datasets corresponding to grades 1 through 8. Each grade-specific dataset contains 100 multiple-choice questions, balanced across the relevant skill domains for that grade. Every question was artificially converted into a four-option multiple-choice format (A–D), where distractors were either numerically close to the correct answer or conceptually plausible alternatives. The balanced distribution across skills ensures that per-skill evaluation is statistically stable and that no single skill disproportionately influences aggregate metrics.
During preprocessing and evaluation, certain items were identified as systematically ambiguous or poorly constructed due to repeated cross-model errors potentially stemming from the limited mathematical reasoning capabilities of current LLMs rather than the intended skill gap. These items were replaced with alternatives from the same educational standard while preserving skill balance. This quality-control stage ensured that subsequent forgetting behavior could be attributed to controlled skill manipulation rather than dataset artifacts.
Per-skill mastery is computed using the accuracy per skill vector, defined as the number of correct responses associated with a skill divided by the number of items tagged with that skill. This representation enables direct comparison between the intended binary mastery vector and the observed performance profile.

5. Methodology
5.0 Baseline: The Perfect Student Upper Bound
Before introducing controlled forgetting, it is necessary to establish whether modern language models can reliably simulate a perfect student. A Perfect Student is defined as a generative agent whose mastery vector is K = {1,1,1,...,1}, implying full mastery across all skills. This configuration serves as an upper performance bound and validates that any later degradation in accuracy originates from deliberate skill suppression rather than inherent model incapacity.
Multiple state-of-the-art language models were evaluated under this full-mastery configuration. Across grade-level datasets, several frontier models achieved performance exceeding 95% accuracy, demonstrating that near-perfect curriculum-aligned behavior is attainable. This baseline provides both a performance ceiling and methodological confidence that subsequent errors in imperfect configurations reflect controlled knowledge gaps rather than systemic failure.

5.1 Generative Student Definition
The overall architecture of the controllable generative student framework is illustrated in Figure 1, which summarizes the full pipeline from skill-vector specification to LLM answer generation. The figure presents the pipeline that transforms a predefined skill vector into a structured prompt used to condition the language model. The process begins with a binary skill vector k = {1,0,...,1}, where each position corresponds to a Knowledge Component (KC). A value of 1 indicates that the student has mastered the KC, while 0 indicates that the KC is intentionally removed in order to simulate a knowledge gap. The vector is translated into explicit skill categories and embedded into a structured student profile prompt that instructs the large language model to behave according to the specified knowledge state. The prompt is then provided to the LLM, which generates an answer prediction for the given question, simulating the behavior of a student with the defined skill profile.

5.2 Model Selection
This study evaluates three state-of-the-art large language models representing different architectural and training characteristics:
Claude (Anthropic) is a safety-aligned model known for strong instruction-following capabilities and stable reasoning behavior.
DeepSeek is a high-performance model optimized for reasoning tasks, often demonstrating strong mathematical capabilities but reduced compliance with behavioral constraints.
GPT-4o (OpenAI) is a widely used general-purpose model with strong overall performance, but known to exhibit trade-offs between instruction following and reasoning robustness.
The selection of these models enables comparison across different design philosophies, including alignment strength, reasoning capacity, and susceptibility to prompt-based behavioral control.
Importantly, all selected models were evaluated on the MathCAMPS dataset and were chosen only after demonstrating strong baseline performance. This ensures that each model is capable of accurately solving the tasks prior to introducing controlled skill suppression, and that any observed performance degradation is a result of the experimental manipulation rather than inherent model limitations.
5.3 Prompting Strategies
Three prompting strategies were implemented within a unified prompt structure. The rule-based strategy provides explicit behavioral constraints instructing the model to answer correctly when a skill is mastered and to produce consistent mistakes when the skill is missing. The few-shot strategy supplies skill-aligned demonstration examples that illustrate both correct and incorrect behavior patterns. To prevent data leakage, few-shot examples are retrieved from an external file fully separated from the evaluation datasets. The combined strategy integrates explicit behavioral rules with concrete demonstrations to reinforce consistency and realism.
Each generated prompt follows a consistent architecture that includes the student’s mastered and missing skills, behavioral instructions and/or examples, and the current multiple-choice question. The model is instructed to return exactly one answer option without explanation, ensuring standardized output suitable for quantitative analysis.
5.4 Experimental Protocol
The overall experimental pipeline is illustrated in Figure 2. The experiment begins with the construction of a set of simulated students, each defined by a distinct mastery vector. These students represent different combinations of retained and removed skills and therefore correspond to different learning-gap profiles. Each simulated student is evaluated on a test composed of skill-tagged questions drawn from the grade-specific datasets.
For every student profile, the full question set is presented to multiple language models. Each model receives the student-specific prompt that encodes the corresponding mastery vector and generates answer predictions for all questions. The outputs are then collected and passed to the evaluation stage, where performance is aggregated by skill domain and compared with the intended mastery vector.
This pipeline allows systematic comparison across three dimensions: student profiles (1…n), skill-tagged questions (1…m), and language models (1…i). The resulting responses form the basis for controllability evaluation, including per-skill accuracy, relative loss, RMSE, and cross-skill influence analysis.

5.5 Evaluation Metrics
Evaluation operates at the student–model pair level, aggregated across all test items. Per-skill accuracy is computed and compared to the original binary mastery vector. Relative loss and the controllability score quantify selective forgetting and retention behavior, while RMSE measures overall deviation between intended and observed skill profiles.
In addition to these scalar metrics, the framework incorporates a Cross-Skill Influence Matrix. For each simulated configuration in which a specific skill is deactivated, performance changes are measured not only on the targeted skill (diagonal elements) but also on all other skills (off-diagonal elements). The diagonal entries quantify direct forgetting effectiveness, whereas off-diagonal entries capture unintended cross-skill interference. A robust prompting mechanism should localize performance degradation to the intentionally removed skill while minimizing collateral impact on retained competencies. This matrix therefore provides a structural evaluation of whether the model simulates independent competencies or exhibits entangled knowledge representations.
Together, these metrics measure how accurately the model reflects the intended student profile, how effectively prompting enforces selective forgetting, and whether induced knowledge gaps remain isolated at the skill level rather than propagating across unrelated domains.

6. Experiments
The experimental pipeline consists of four stages. First, the Perfect Student configuration establishes the upper-bound performance for each grade-level dataset, ensuring that the evaluated models are capable of solving the tasks when no skill constraints are applied. Second, controlled skill forgetting experiments systematically deactivate selected skills in order to evaluate the model’s ability to suppress specific knowledge while retaining others. Third, prompt ablation experiments compare rule-based, few-shot, and combined prompting strategies under identical student profiles, isolating the effect of prompt design on controllability. Fourth, variance analysis examines stability across repeated executions.
To ensure comprehensive coverage of possible student knowledge states, we simulate all possible combinations of skill mastery profiles. For a given grade with K skills, this results in  distinct student configurations, where each configuration is represented as a binary skill vector , with 1 indicating a mastered skill and 0 indicating a forgotten skill. This exhaustive enumeration enables systematic evaluation of controllability across the full space of possible imperfect students.
All experiments are conducted with temperature set to 0, ensuring deterministic model behavior and eliminating stochastic variation due to sampling. This allows us to attribute differences in performance directly to prompt design and model characteristics rather than randomness.
The experimental evaluation is performed on two grade-level datasets, Grade 4 and Grade 5, each containing a fixed set of skill domains and a balanced distribution of questions per skill. Experiments are executed independently for each grade to preserve internal consistency in skill definitions and difficulty levels.
All experiments are conducted across three different large language models—Anthropic’s Claude, DeepSeek, and OpenAI’s GPT-4o—in order to compare controllability across different architectures, training regimes, and alignment characteristics.
Results are aggregated at multiple levels: per skill, per student profile, and per model. This multi-level aggregation enables both fine-grained analysis of individual skill behavior and higher-level comparisons across models and prompting strategies. This exhaustive setup ensures that the evaluation captures both best-case and worst-case controllability scenarios.

7.1 Prompt Strategy Comparison
Before analyzing model behavior in terms of controllability, it is essential to evaluate the effectiveness of the prompting strategies used to impose the intended skill profiles. As the entire framework relies on the model’s ability to follow a predefined mastery vector, prompt design becomes a critical factor in determining whether selective forgetting can be achieved in practice.  To quantify this, we measure the Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) between the intended skill vector and the observed per-skill accuracy. Lower RMSE indicates better alignment between the desired and actual behavior.  The results show a clear and consistent pattern across both Grade 4 and Grade 5. The combined prompting strategy (rules + few-shot examples) significantly outperforms both the rule-based and few-shot approaches. Specifically, the combined strategy achieves low RMSE values (≈0.08–0.12), indicating strong alignment with the intended mastery vector and accurate simulation of the desired student profile.   In contrast, the few-shot strategy alone results in substantially higher RMSE (≈0.45–0.60), suggesting that examples without explicit constraints are insufficient to enforce structured forgetting. Similarly, the rule-based strategy also exhibits high RMSE (≈0.50–0.60), indicating that instructions alone are not robust enough to override the model’s internal knowledge.   These findings highlight a key insight: neither explicit instructions nor examples alone are sufficient to control model behavior reliably. Instead, their combination creates a complementary effect—rules define the desired behavior explicitly, while examples demonstrate how this behavior should manifest in practice. This synergy enables the model to better internalize and apply the intended skll constraints.  Overall, this analysis establishes that prompt design is a necessary condition for controllability. The combined strategy provides a stable and effective mechanism for enforcing selective forgetting, and therefore serves as the foundation for all subsequent experiments presented in this section.

However, this behavior is not uniform across all skills. The variation may stem from differences in intrinsic task difficulty, the structure of the questions, or biases in the model’s pretraining that make some reasoning patterns harder to override.

7.3 Retained vs. Forgotten 
Across both Grade 4 and Grade 5, a clear and consistent separation emerges between retained and forgotten skills. For retained skills, the imperfect student maintains accuracy levels close to the perfect student (≈0.85–0.92), indicating that knowledge is largely preserved when required. This behavior is consistent across all models, including OpenAI’s GPT-4o, which demonstrates stable retention comparable to Anthropic’s Claude and DeepSeek. In contrast, for forgotten skills, accuracy drops substantially, but with notable differences across models: Claude exhibits the strongest forgetting effect (≈0.05–0.15), achieving near-complete suppression of the targeted skills; DeepSeek shows weaker forgetting (≈0.30–0.40), indicating partial resistance to the imposed constraints; and GPT-4o falls in between, demonstrating clear and consistent forgetting (≈0.20–0.35), but not to the same extent as Claude, suggesting moderate controllability. Importantly, this degradation is not global but highly localized to the specified skills, confirming that the behavior reflects controlled and selective forgetting rather than general performance decline. The magnitude and consistency of the gap between retained and forgotten skills indicate that this effect is not due to random variation or prompt instability, but rather a structured and reproducible response to the imposed skill constraints. This separation is also visually evident in the per-skill comparison plots, where retained skills cluster near the perfect-student baseline, while forgotten skills form a distinct low-accuracy regime. Together, these findings provide strong empirical evidence that LLMs can be controlled at the level of individual skills, enabling the generation of imperfect students with targeted and interpretable knowledge gaps.;

8. Discussion
The results demonstrate that controllability is not an inherent property of LLMs, but rather an emergent behavior that arises from the interaction between prompting strategy and model architecture. In particular, effective control requires both a sufficiently expressive prompt design and a model that is capable of adhering to externally imposed constraints.
The weak inter-skill correlations observed across grades support the core design assumption of independent skill units. This independence enables targeted manipulation of specific skills without inducing widespread degradation in others, which is essential for realistic educational simulation. In real learning scenarios, students typically exhibit localized misconceptions rather than uniform failure, and the results confirm that such behavior can be approximated within LLM-based systems.
However, the variability across models highlights a key limitation. Strongly pretrained models may resist forgetting, even when explicitly instructed to do so. For example, DeepSeek consistently retains substantial residual knowledge, suggesting that prompt-based control alone may be insufficient to fully override internal representations. Similarly, the behavior observed in GPT-4o indicates a different limitation: partial compliance accompanied by unintended degradation of retained skills, reflecting a trade-off between instruction-following and stability.
These findings suggest that controllability is constrained not only by prompt design but also by intrinsic model properties. As a result, achieving reliable and precise control requires alignment between the prompting strategy and the model’s internal behavior.
From an analytical perspective, several key dimensions emerge: whether forgetting can be fully enforced or remains partially suppressed by pretraining, whether increased prompt structure improves compliance, whether certain skill domains are inherently more resistant to forgetting, and how controllability generalizes across grade levels and task distributions.
Finally, this study is subject to several limitations. The framework relies exclusively on prompt-based control without incorporating fine-tuning or training-time interventions, assumes a binary mastery representation, and treats each question as associated with a single skill. These simplifications enable controlled experimentation but may limit the realism of the simulated students.
.

9. Future Work
Future work will extend this framework in several directions. First, modeling skill dependencies using a hierarchical or graph-based representation could capture more realistic relationships between concepts. Second, replacing the binary mastery assumption with a continuous scale would allow for more nuanced student modeling. Third, reinforcement learning or fine-tuning approaches could be explored to improve alignment and achieve stronger and more reliable control beyond prompt-based methods.
In addition, future work will expand the experimental setup to cover a broader range of grade levels, enabling analysis of how controllability scales across different levels of difficulty and cognitive complexity. This includes extending the framework to additional grades within the mathematics domain.
Further directions include enabling dynamic forgetting during multi-step reasoning processes, and extending the framework to additional subject domains such as science, language, history, and social studies, in order to support more comprehensive educational simulations.

10. Conclusion
This master’s project demonstrates that structured, skill-level controllability in LLM-based student simulation is both measurable and achievable. The proposed framework provides a principled approach for evaluating selective compliance, enabling fine-grained analysis of how models retain and suppress knowledge at the level of individual skills.
The results validate the feasibility of generating controllable imperfect students with interpretable knowledge gaps, supporting applications in teacher training and classroom simulation. At the same time, they highlight the importance of prompt design and model selection in achieving reliable control.
Overall, this work establishes a foundational methodology for controlled deployment of LLMs in educational settings, and opens the door for future systems that combine prompting, training, and structured representations to achieve more robust and scalable controllability.
